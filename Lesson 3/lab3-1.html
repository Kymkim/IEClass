Space Shuttle Tiles

The Space Shuttles, which were the longest running space vehicle program in history, was finally retired in 2011 leaving the United States with no means to put humans into space by our own accord. When an object from space enters the Earth’s atmosphere, it encounters extreme temperatures usually resulting in the object being completely incinerated, and for the Space Shuttles, this would not be good news for the astronauts returning home.

To protect the shuttle and astronauts, each shuttle was covered with more than33,000 reusable heat insulation tiles. The tiles were made of foam glass, each was custom made for their specific positions on the Space Shuttle. There were two types of tiles used on the shuttle, low heat and high heat. The white tiles are low heat resistant and the black tiles are high heat resistant tiles because they endure the brunt of the heat from re-entry into the Earth's atmosphere and can withstand heat up to 2300 degrees.

One of the greatest benefits of the shuttle tiles is their reusability. Earlier spacecraft, such as Apollo missions used ablative heat shields which could only be used once at a cost of $30,000 per square foot. The shuttle tiles can be reused up to 100 missions at a cost of $10,000 per square foot.

Each shuttle tile is identified with a unique code or serial number, and NASA keeps track of each tile’s, history so engineers know when the tile was added, which missions it has been on, and when it should be replaced.
